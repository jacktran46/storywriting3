## C081 | Aunt's Locket Mocked at Reception → Portrait Reveals Missing Sister

**Source:** Concept 01 #16 — The Rose Gold Locket  
**Opening style:** Dialogue slap  
**Setting:** Fairmont Olympic Hotel grand ballroom, Seattle, Washington — Saturday 7pm, crystal chandelier light, rain streaking tall windows, 180 guests at round tables

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** The groom's wealthy aunt, Constance Hargrove, loudly mocks the bride's "cheap little necklace" in front of the head table during toasts, calling it costume jewelry unfit for the Hargrove name and demanding the bride remove it before photographs.
- **Clue:** The bride's hand goes to the rose gold locket at her throat; the clasp pops open under pressure and a tiny oval portrait inside catches the chandelier light — a young woman's face, painted in miniature, that the bride has never been able to identify.
- **Turn:** Constance's champagne flute slips from her fingers and shatters on the marble floor; she has gone white; the portrait is her younger sister Vivian, who vanished from the family in 1991 and was declared dead by the estate.
- **Payoff:** The bride, Mara Chen-Hargrove, was adopted at three weeks old from a Portland agency in 1991; Vivian Hargrove had surrendered her in secret to protect the infant from the family patriarch's will clause that stripped inheritance from any daughter who bore a child out of wedlock; Vivian painted the miniature herself and sealed it in the locket before handing the baby over; she died of cancer in 2004 without reuniting; the adoption file Constance's attorney had sealed for 33 years is unsealed by court order within 60 days; Mara inherits Vivian's full share; Constance loses control of the estate trust.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Constance in emerald gown with champagne flute mid-slip from her fingers, face gone white; bride Mara in ivory beaded gown with one hand at her throat holding the open rose gold locket toward the light; groom beside Mara half-standing from his chair with napkin still in hand; head-table guests frozen mid-toast; rain streaking tall windows behind.

**Character Lock:**
- Mara Chen-Hargrove (bride) — 33, East Asian features, dark hair in low chignon, ivory beaded wedding gown, rose gold locket on delicate chain at her throat, calm defiant eyes
- Constance Hargrove (groom's aunt) — 67, silver updo, emerald silk gown, pearl earrings, champagne flute slipping from manicured fingers, face drained white
- Julian Hargrove (groom) — 35, classic black tuxedo, half-standing from chair, protective hand reaching toward Mara
- Setting — Fairmont Olympic Hotel grand ballroom, Seattle WA, Saturday 7pm, crystal chandeliers, round tables with white linens, rain streaking tall windows
- Object clue — rose gold locket with miniature painted portrait inside (pays off Act 3–4)

## C082 | Heiress Trapped in Elevator → Cleaner's Lullaby Reveals Mother's Voice

**Source:** Concept 02 #3 — The Elevator Calm  
**Opening style:** Room goes wrong  
**Setting:** The Peninsula Hotel service elevator, Chicago, Illinois — Wednesday 11pm, emergency lighting amber, mirrored walls, stuck between floors 14 and 15

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** Hotel heiress Sloane Whitfield panics during a sudden elevator stop between floors; an elderly cleaning woman in a gray uniform is the only other person in the car; Sloane screams at her to do something, calls her useless.
- **Clue:** The cleaner begins humming a soft lullaby to calm Sloane — three descending notes repeated, a melody Sloane has heard exactly once before, on a warped cassette tape her adoptive mother kept locked in a bedside drawer.
- **Turn:** Sloane's panic collapses into a different kind of stillness; she whispers "Where did you learn that?" and the cleaner answers with Sloane's birth name — "Amelia Rose" — a name no one alive should know.
- **Payoff:** The cleaner is Sloane's biological mother, Dolores Vega, who surrendered her at birth in 1992 after the Whitfield family's attorney threatened to have Dolores deported if she did not sign away rights; Dolores took the hotel cleaning job in 2019 hoping to see her daughter one floor at a time; the cassette tape was a recording Dolores made for the baby the night before signing; the Whitfield trust is revealed to have falsified the original adoption petition; Sloane files for legal name restoration and establishes a fund in Dolores's name.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Sloane pressed against mirrored elevator wall in cocktail dress, mascara streaked, phone with no signal held up; Dolores in gray hotel-cleaning uniform kneeling calmly with mop cart behind her, lips parted in a hum, one weathered hand extended palm-up toward Sloane; amber emergency light casting warm shadows; elevator buttons dark.

**Character Lock:**
- Sloane Whitfield — 32, blonde bob disheveled, black cocktail dress, heels kicked off, mascara streaked, pressed against mirrored wall, phone raised with no signal
- Dolores Vega — 63, dark skin, gray-streaked black hair in a low bun under a hairnet, gray Peninsula hotel-cleaning uniform, name tag "DOLORES," weathered hands, calm steady eyes, kneeling
- Setting — The Peninsula Hotel service elevator, Chicago IL, Wednesday 11pm, stuck between floors 14–15, amber emergency lighting, mirrored walls, mop cart in corner, elevator buttons dark
- Object clue — warped cassette tape (referenced in story; visual cue is Dolores's humming posture + name tag) (pays off Act 3–4)

## C083 | Curator Insults Museum Cleaner → She Painted the Original

**Source:** Concept 03 #13 — The Museum Cleaner  
**Opening style:** Accuser POV  
**Setting:** Smithsonian American Art Museum restoration gallery, Washington, D.C. — Thursday 2pm, track-lit white walls, one large canvas on an easel under conservation lamps, staff-only area

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** Chief curator Garrett Niles catches an elderly cleaner standing too close to a valuable 1967 oil painting during a private donor walkthrough; he publicly berates her for touching the frame and threatens termination in front of six donors.
- **Clue:** The cleaner calmly points at the lower-left quadrant of the canvas and says, quietly, "The restoration got the green wrong — it was viridian, not sap." One donor, a retired art conservator, freezes mid-step.
- **Turn:** The retired conservator pulls up the museum's own provenance file on her tablet; the original artist is listed as "Anonymous, attributed to D.C. School, 1967"; she asks the cleaner to describe the brushwork technique on the sky — and the cleaner describes a method not documented anywhere in the museum's records.
- **Payoff:** The cleaner is Lorraine Mays, the actual painter, a Black woman who in 1967 could not exhibit under her own name; her white gallery dealer took credit and sold the work anonymously; Lorraine lost all documentation in a 1972 apartment fire; she took the museum cleaning job six years ago to be near the painting she was never credited for; the conservator's provenance audit confirms her claim within 90 days; the painting is re-attributed; Niles is reassigned from public-facing duties; Lorraine receives a retrospective exhibition.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Curator Niles in navy blazer pointing aggressively at Lorraine; Lorraine in khaki museum-cleaning smock standing calm with one finger extended toward the lower-left corner of the large canvas; six donors in business attire frozen mid-walkthrough; track lighting casting sharp shadows on white gallery walls; retired conservator with tablet half-raised.

**Character Lock:**
- Lorraine Mays — 79, deep brown skin, silver hair in a short natural, khaki museum-cleaning smock with ID badge, reading glasses on a beaded chain at her chest, one finger extended toward the canvas, calm dignified posture
- Garrett Niles (chief curator) — 51, navy blazer, tortoiseshell glasses, aggressive pointing posture, flushed face
- Dr. Helen Park (retired conservator/donor) — 64, charcoal pantsuit, tablet in hand, frozen mid-step
- Setting — Smithsonian American Art Museum restoration gallery, Washington D.C., Thursday 2pm, track-lit white walls, large 1967 oil painting on conservation easel under lamps, staff-only rope visible
- Object clue — specific brushwork technique + color correction only the original artist would know (pays off Act 3–4)

## C084 | Homeless Man's Patchwork Blanket → Veteran Recognizes Brother's Unit Patch

**Source:** Concept 04 #9 — The Military Patch Quilt  
**Opening style:** Visual reveal  
**Setting:** Union Station main hall, Washington, D.C. — Friday 6am, pre-dawn gray light through arched skylights, marble floors, homeless man sleeping on a bench near the east entrance

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** A transit police officer nudges a sleeping homeless man to move along before commuters arrive; a passing Army captain in dress greens stops to buy coffee at the kiosk and glances at the bench.
- **Clue:** The homeless man's patchwork blanket shifts as he stirs — one square sewn into the corner is a faded 3rd Infantry Division "Rock of the Marne" combat patch with a unique hand-stitched modification only one platoon used: a small gold thread star added after a classified 2004 Fallujah extraction.
- **Turn:** The captain drops his coffee. He crosses the hall at double-time and kneels beside the bench without speaking to the officer; he lifts the blanket corner and reads a name inked on the back of the patch in permanent marker.
- **Payoff:** The homeless man is Staff Sergeant Elijah Boone, the captain's older brother, MIA-presumed-dead since 2006 after a PTSD-related disappearance from Walter Reed; the family held a memorial; the gold-star patch was sewn by their mother before deployment; Elijah has lived unidentified on the streets for 18 years; the captain initiates VA reactivation, family reunion, and housing within the week; the transit officer is formally counseled for failing to check veteran-ID protocols.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Captain Marcus Boone in Army dress greens kneeling at the bench, coffee cup dropped and spilling on marble, one hand lifting the blanket corner to reveal the faded combat patch with gold thread star; Elijah Boone half-sitting up with matted beard and layered coats, confused eyes meeting his brother's; transit officer standing two steps back with hand on radio, uncertain; gray pre-dawn skylight overhead.

**Character Lock:**
- Staff Sgt. Elijah Boone — 44, gaunt face, matted dark beard, layered coats over a faded Army PT shirt, patchwork blanket with unit patch in corner, confused waking eyes
- Captain Marcus Boone — 38, Army dress greens with ribbons and captain's bars, close-cropped hair, tears forming, kneeling with one hand on blanket corner, dropped coffee cup at his knee
- Officer Daniels (transit police) — 29, navy uniform, hand on shoulder radio, standing two steps back, uncertain expression
- Setting — Union Station main hall, Washington D.C., Friday 6am, gray pre-dawn light through arched skylights, marble floors, wooden bench near east entrance, coffee kiosk in background
- Object clue — faded 3rd Infantry Division combat patch with gold thread star + permanent-marker name on back (pays off Act 3–4)

## C085 | Guests Laugh at Child's One-String Violin → Conductor Hears Lost Mentor's Composition

**Source:** Concept 05 #4 — The One-String Violin  
**Opening style:** Victim restraint  
**Setting:** Lincoln Center plaza outside David Geffen Hall, New York City — Saturday 7pm, autumn evening, golden lobby light spilling onto limestone steps, gala guests arriving in evening wear

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** A 10-year-old girl in a too-large coat plays a battered violin with one remaining string on the plaza steps while gala guests file past; a donor couple laughs loudly, the husband flicking a penny toward her open case and calling it "rat music."
- **Clue:** Maestro Alejandro Vidal, arriving late, stops on the third step; the melody the girl plays on one string is a 16-bar passage from a composition his late mentor, Professor Einar Svensson, never published — a piece that existed only in a single handwritten manuscript lost after the professor's death in 2011.
- **Turn:** Vidal kneels beside the girl on the cold limestone and asks where she learned the piece; she pulls a folded, coffee-stained page of handwritten sheet music from inside the violin case lining.
- **Payoff:** The girl, Iris Svensson, is Einar's granddaughter; her mother (Einar's estranged daughter) died in 2020; Iris ended up in foster care with only the violin and the hidden manuscript page; she taught herself the melody by ear from the one page; Vidal authenticates the manuscript, premieres the full work with the Philharmonic the following season credited to Einar Svensson, and establishes a permanent scholarship trust for Iris; the donor couple's corporate box is quietly revoked.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Iris in oversized coat playing one-string violin on limestone steps, eyes closed, small open case with a few coins; donor husband in tuxedo mid-flick of a penny with smug expression, wife laughing beside him in fur wrap; Maestro Vidal frozen mid-step on the third stair in his black overcoat, one gloved hand at his chest, face stricken; golden lobby light spilling from glass doors behind.

**Character Lock:**
- Iris Svensson — 10, pale skin, tangled strawberry-blonde hair, oversized olive coat too long at the sleeves, battered violin with one string, eyes closed while playing, small open case at her feet
- Donor husband (Craig Pollard) — 55, black tuxedo, red pocket square, mid-penny-flick, smug face
- Donor wife (Vanessa Pollard) — 52, fur wrap over silver gown, laughing with champagne in hand
- Maestro Alejandro Vidal — 61, black wool overcoat, silver scarf, salt-pepper hair, one gloved hand at his chest, stricken expression, frozen on third step
- Setting — Lincoln Center plaza outside David Geffen Hall, NYC, Saturday 7pm, autumn evening, golden lobby light spilling onto limestone steps, gala guests in evening wear arriving
- Object clue — folded coffee-stained page of handwritten sheet music hidden inside violin case lining (pays off Act 3–4)

## C086 | Poor Girl Slashes Billionaire's Portrait → Older Painting Underneath

**Source:** Concept 06 #5 — The Portrait Slash  
**Opening style:** Deadline pressure  
**Setting:** Christie's auction house main salesroom, New York City — Wednesday 8pm, auction in progress, lot 47 of 52, a large oil portrait on the display easel under spotlight, 200 bidders in seats

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** With three lots remaining and a hammer estimate of $4.2M, a young woman in a thrift-store dress pushes past security and slashes a vertical line through a billionaire collector's prized portrait with a box cutter before anyone can stop her; gasps fill the room; security tackles her.
- **Clue:** The slash reveals a second, older canvas layered beneath the portrait — visible brushwork in a distinctive impasto style; paint chips fall and show colors inconsistent with the top painting's palette; a senior appraiser in the third row stands with his hand over his mouth.
- **Turn:** The appraiser identifies the hidden brushwork as consistent with the style of Alma Delgado, a Harlem Renaissance painter whose entire body of work was presumed destroyed in a 1968 gallery fire; the girl shouts from the floor, "That's my grandmother's painting underneath!"
- **Payoff:** The billionaire's family acquired the portrait in 1970 from a dealer who painted over Alma Delgado's originals to sell the canvases cheaply; the girl, Nina Delgado, found her grandmother's studio log listing this specific canvas and its sale to that dealer; Alma Delgado painted 14 works, all presumed lost; the slash reveals the first recovered piece; Christie's halts the auction; provenance investigation identifies 6 more Delgado canvases hidden under other portraits in private collections; Nina's family receives full restitution and the works are exhibited at the Studio Museum in Harlem.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Nina in thrift-store floral dress pinned to the floor by two security guards, box cutter skidding away on polished wood; the slashed portrait on the easel with a vertical tear revealing older colorful brushwork beneath; senior appraiser standing in the third row with hand over mouth; 200 bidders in seats turning toward the commotion; auction spotlight still on the damaged canvas.

**Character Lock:**
- Nina Delgado — 24, brown skin, natural curls pulled back with a scarf, thrift-store floral dress, scuffed boots, pinned to the floor by security, defiant tear-streaked face
- Senior appraiser (Dr. Franklin Osei) — 68, gray beard, charcoal suit, standing in third row, hand over mouth, eyes locked on the revealed canvas
- Two security guards — dark suits, pinning Nina to polished hardwood floor
- Setting — Christie's main salesroom, NYC, Wednesday 8pm, auction in progress, 200 bidders seated, display easel under single spotlight, polished hardwood floor, numbered paddles on laps
- Object clue — older canvas visible through slash with distinctive impasto brushwork + studio log (pays off Act 3–4)

## C087 | Boy Returns Milk Bottles for Coins → Shop Owner Discovers Secret Night Deliveries

**Source:** Concept 07 #11 — The Milk Bottle Note  
**Opening style:** Object clue  
**Setting:** Garrison's Corner Market, Savannah, Georgia — Tuesday 7am, early morning humidity, screen door propped open, wooden floor, refrigerated case humming

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** Twelve-year-old Danny Okafor walks into Garrison's with a wire crate of eight empty glass milk bottles, asking for the deposit refund; shop owner Marvin Garrison sighs and nearly refuses because the boy comes every single morning and the deposit total is barely $2.40.
- **Clue:** Marvin notices a folded thank-you note tucked inside one of the bottles — handwritten, shaky cursive: "Danny — God bless you, son. I wouldn't eat without you. — Mrs. B, Apt 4C." Marvin pulls out a second note from another bottle: "To the boy with the milk — you are the only visitor I have. — Mr. Aldridge, Room 12."
- **Turn:** Marvin follows Danny after the boy leaves; Danny takes a gallon of milk purchased with the deposit money and splits it into small containers, then delivers them door-to-door to six elderly residents in the Eastside Towers housing project who cannot leave their apartments.
- **Payoff:** Danny has been running this route every morning for two years since his grandmother died in the same building; he collects bottles from restaurant recycling bins, returns them for deposit, buys milk, and delivers to seniors his grandmother once cared for; Marvin contacts the Savannah Morning News; the story runs; a local dairy co-op establishes a permanent free-delivery program for homebound seniors; Danny receives a full scholarship to a Savannah prep school; Marvin never charges him for bottles again.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Danny at the wooden counter holding the wire crate of empty milk bottles; Marvin behind the counter with one bottle held up to the light, squinting at the folded note inside; screen door propped open with morning humidity haze; worn wooden floor; refrigerated case glowing in background.

**Character Lock:**
- Danny Okafor — 12, dark brown skin, close-cropped hair, oversized hand-me-down t-shirt tucked into belted shorts, worn sneakers, wire crate of glass milk bottles in both hands, earnest patient face
- Marvin Garrison (shop owner) — 58, gray mustache, white grocer's apron over flannel shirt, reading glasses pushed up on forehead, one bottle held to the light, squinting at the note inside
- Setting — Garrison's Corner Market, Savannah GA, Tuesday 7am, early morning humidity, screen door propped open, worn wooden floor, refrigerated dairy case humming, hand-lettered price signs (angled away)
- Object clue — handwritten thank-you notes inside returned milk bottles (pays off Act 3–4)

## C088 | Auctioneer Insults Worn Military Jacket → Retired Admiral Stops the Sale

**Source:** Concept 08 #10 — The Auction Uniform  
**Opening style:** Moral choice  
**Setting:** Harrington Estate Auction barn, Annapolis, Maryland — Saturday 1pm, autumn afternoon, high barn rafters, folding chairs, 60 bidders, military memorabilia lot on the block

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** Auctioneer Vince Harrington holds up a faded, patched Marine dress jacket from a lot of "junk military surplus" and jokes that it "smells like mothballs and failure," drawing laughs from bargain hunters; he starts the bid at $15.
- **Clue:** A white-haired man in the back row stands slowly; he is wearing a plain navy blazer with no insignia, but the woman beside him — his aide — has already pulled out a phone and is dialing; the man's eyes are locked on a small, hand-sewn detail on the jacket's inner collar that the auctioneer has not noticed.
- **Turn:** The man walks to the front without bidding, places his hand flat on the podium, and says, "That jacket belongs to a living hero. You will stop this sale." The barn goes silent.
- **Payoff:** The jacket belonged to Master Sergeant Rosa Vega, USMC, who earned a Navy Cross in 2005 for pulling wounded Marines from a burning vehicle in Ramadi; she lost the jacket when her storage unit was auctioned for unpaid rent during her hospitalization for combat injuries in 2018; the white-haired man is Rear Admiral (ret.) Thomas Garner, who pinned her medal himself; the hand-sewn collar detail is a small embroidered rose — Rosa's mother sewed one into every uniform; Garner purchases the entire lot, returns the jacket to Rosa personally, and funds her medical debt clearance; the auctioneer issues a public apology.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Auctioneer Vince holding up the faded patched Marine dress jacket with mocking expression at the podium; Admiral Garner standing in the back row, plain navy blazer, white hair, jaw set, one hand gripping the folding chair in front of him as if about to move; aide beside him with phone raised to ear; 60 bidders in folding chairs turning to look; autumn light through high barn slats.

**Character Lock:**
- Rear Admiral (ret.) Thomas Garner — 72, white hair, plain navy blazer (no insignia), steel-blue eyes, jaw set, standing from back-row folding chair
- Vince Harrington (auctioneer) — 45, rolled shirtsleeves, microphone headset, holding up faded Marine jacket, mocking grin
- Aide (Lt. Cmdr. Sarah Park, ret.) — 38, dark pantsuit, phone at ear, standing beside Garner
- Setting — Harrington Estate Auction barn, Annapolis MD, Saturday 1pm, autumn afternoon light through high barn slats, folding chairs in rows, podium with microphone, lot table with military items
- Object clue — small hand-sewn embroidered rose on jacket's inner collar (pays off Act 3–4)

## C089 | Bank Manager Refuses Elderly Woman → Chairman Calls Her the Founder

**Source:** Concept 09 #3 — The Old Woman at the Bank  
**Opening style:** Deadline pressure  
**Setting:** First Continental Bank flagship branch, Boston, Massachusetts — Friday 4:45pm, 15 minutes before closing, marble lobby, brass teller cages, line of customers

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** Branch manager Derek Callahan refuses to serve an elderly woman in worn clothes who has been waiting 40 minutes, telling her the branch is closing and she should "try the ATM outside" while escorting VIP clients to the private banking suite.
- **Clue:** The woman calmly sets a small leather folio on the counter; inside is a single sheet of paper — a hand-signed original charter from 1962 with the bank's founding seal and a name: "E. Whitmore, Founding Partner."
- **Turn:** The bank chairman, arriving for a 5pm board dinner in the private suite, sees the woman through the glass partition, stops mid-stride, and crosses the lobby to embrace her — calling her "Mrs. Whitmore" loudly enough for every teller to hear.
- **Payoff:** Eleanor Whitmore, 89, co-founded First Continental in 1962 with her late husband; after his death in 1988, the board slowly marginalized her, removed her portrait from the lobby, and reclassified her shares as non-voting; she has lived modestly on a fixed income while the bank grew to $14B in assets; the charter document proves her founding partnership was never legally dissolved; the chairman (who was mentored by her husband) initiates a board vote to restore her full voting rights and back-dividends; Derek Callahan is terminated that evening; Eleanor's portrait is rehung by Monday morning.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Derek in charcoal suit blocking Eleanor's path to the teller window with one hand raised in a "stop" gesture; Eleanor in worn camel coat holding the open leather folio with the charter document visible; bank chairman frozen mid-stride through the glass partition door in his overcoat, face registering shock; brass teller cages and marble lobby; clock on the wall showing 4:47pm (blurred).

**Character Lock:**
- Eleanor Whitmore — 89, white hair in a soft wave, worn camel coat over a cardigan, sensible shoes, small leather folio with charter document, dignified calm expression
- Derek Callahan (branch manager) — 42, charcoal suit, burgundy tie, gold name badge, one hand raised to block her, dismissive expression
- Chairman Robert Ames — 66, silver hair, navy overcoat over suit, mid-stride through glass partition door, face registering shock and recognition
- Setting — First Continental Bank flagship branch, Boston MA, Friday 4:45pm, marble lobby, brass teller cages, overhead clock, glass partition to private banking suite, line of customers
- Object clue — small leather folio with 1962 hand-signed original charter document (pays off Act 3–4)

## C090 | Cleaner Breaks Picture Frame → Hidden Photo Reveals True Owner

**Source:** Concept 10 #17 — The Broken Frame  
**Opening style:** Silent witness  
**Setting:** Whitmore-Lacey Estate main parlor, Newport, Rhode Island — Sunday 10am, autumn morning light through tall bay windows, antique furniture under dust covers, estate sale prep in progress

**4-beat arc:**
- **Setup:** During estate-sale preparation at a deceased billionaire's Newport mansion, a cleaning woman accidentally knocks a heavy gilt picture frame off the mantel; the estate executor screams at her and threatens to dock her pay for the damage.
- **Clue:** The frame cracks on the marble hearth and the backing separates, revealing a second photograph hidden behind the visible portrait — a faded 1958 black-and-white photo of a young Black woman in a nurse's uniform holding a newborn on the mansion's front steps, with a handwritten inscription on the back.
- **Turn:** The cleaning woman picks up the hidden photo, reads the inscription, and goes very still; the estate's attorney, arriving for the 10:30 walkthrough, sees the photo in her hand and asks to read the inscription aloud: "To my daughter Cecilia, born here May 3, 1958. Your mother, H. Whitmore-Lacey."
- **Payoff:** The billionaire patriarch had a daughter with his Black nurse, Helen, in 1958; he kept the birth secret, sent Helen away with severance and a sealed NDA, and raised no other biological children; Cecilia was the cleaning woman's mother, who died in 2019 without knowing her father's identity; the cleaning woman, Janelle Whitmore (she kept the name), is the direct granddaughter and sole biological heir; the handwritten inscription and hidden photo override the existing will's charitable trust clause; Janelle inherits the estate; the executor who screamed at her is replaced by the attorney on the spot.

**Peak-tension moment (photo):** Cleaning woman Janelle kneeling on the marble hearth holding the faded hidden photograph, eyes wide with recognition; estate executor standing over her with finger pointing down in mid-shout; cracked gilt frame on the floor with backing separated; estate attorney frozen in the parlor doorway with briefcase in hand; tall bay windows with autumn morning light; dust-covered antique furniture.

**Character Lock:**
- Janelle Whitmore — 34, brown skin, dark curly hair tied back with a bandana, cleaning company polo and jeans, kneeling on marble hearth, faded 1958 photo in both hands, eyes wide
- Bradford Lacey III (estate executor) — 59, tweed blazer, standing over her, finger pointing down, red-faced mid-shout
- Attorney Meredith Holt — 47, navy coat, leather briefcase, frozen in parlor doorway, hand on door frame
- Setting — Whitmore-Lacey Estate main parlor, Newport RI, Sunday 10am, autumn morning light through tall bay windows, marble hearth with fireplace, antique furniture under dust covers, cracked gilt frame on floor
- Object clue — hidden 1958 photograph with handwritten inscription on back (pays off Act 3–4)
